Banks Won't Be Disrupted by Crypto. They'll Be Disrupted by Banks That Tokenized.

The existential threat to incumbent financial institutions was never DeFi. It was always the competitor that understood tokenization as infrastructure, not as a product.
March 1, 2025 · Bruno Grossi

For a decade, the disruption narrative in financial services pointed outward. Crypto-native protocols would disintermediate banks. DeFi would replace prime brokers. Stablecoins would end correspondent banking. The threat came from outside the industry, from systems that didn’t need banks at all.

That narrative was wrong — not because the technology was wrong, but because it misidentified the vector.

The institutions most threatened by tokenization are not threatened by crypto. They are threatened by other banks.

Why the Crypto Disruption Story Didn’t Land#

The premise of crypto-as-disruptor required something that hasn’t happened: institutional trust at scale without a regulated wrapper.

DeFi protocols are extraordinarily efficient at certain things — permissionless liquidity, programmable settlement, transparent collateralization. They are extraordinarily bad at other things — KYC, legal recourse, compliance with capital requirements, fiduciary accountability. For retail speculation, those deficiencies are acceptable. For a pension fund settling a $2 billion position, they are not.

Crypto-native finance and regulated finance are not competing for the same customer at the same moment. The disruption thesis assumed convergence that hasn’t come, and may not come in any near-term timeframe that matters for strategic planning.

What has come, quietly and without much fanfare, is tokenization inside regulated institutions. And that changes everything.

Tokenization Is Not a Product. It Is Infrastructure.#

This distinction is where most strategic analysis fails.

When BlackRock launched its BUIDL fund — a tokenized money market fund now holding approximately $1.8 billion in assets — the story was framed as a product launch. A new way for institutions to access short-term yield. That framing is too narrow.

BUIDL is infrastructure. It demonstrated that a regulated, institutional-grade financial product could be represented on a blockchain, with 24/7 settlement, programmable transfer restrictions, and automated yield distribution — without sacrificing any of the compliance properties that institutional investors require. The product was the proof of concept. The infrastructure is the point.

The same logic applies to JPMorgan’s Onyx, to tokenized repo markets settling in minutes instead of days, to the wave of tokenized treasury products now processing billions in weekly volume. These are not experiments. They are the early operational layer of a different kind of financial system — one built on the same institutions, the same regulatory frameworks, the same fiduciary structures, but with a radically different cost basis and product surface area.

What Banks That Tokenize Actually Gain#

Settlement latency is the obvious one. T+2 to T+0, or near-zero, meaningfully changes capital efficiency for every institution on both sides of a trade. The less obvious gains are more consequential.

Programmable money expands the product surface area. A tokenized deposit is not just a faster deposit. It is a deposit that can carry conditions — release on delivery of an asset, automatic compliance checks at transfer, yield that compounds and distributes without manual intervention. Every financial product built on programmable money can do things that paper-based contracts and batch-settlement systems cannot.

Composability creates distribution without intermediaries. A bank that tokenizes its credit products can make them available across any platform that speaks the same protocol. This isn’t disintermediation — it is distribution at zero marginal cost. The bank retains the credit relationship, the regulatory standing, the customer. It gains reach it would otherwise have to build or buy.

CBDC interoperability is not optional. In Brazil, Real Digital — the central bank’s digital currency — operates on a DLT infrastructure. The institutions that integrate with that infrastructure early gain settlement access, product integration possibilities, and operational familiarity that latecomers will have to buy at a premium. Every major CBDC program creates the same dynamic. Banks that tokenize are already speaking the language. Banks that didn’t will need a translator.

The Actual Threat Model#

Incumbent banks face a cost structure built for a world that is being systematically re-engineered.

Correspondent banking margins exist because cross-border settlement is slow and opaque. Custodial fees exist because asset transfers require manual reconciliation. Trade finance margins exist because verifying the chain of title on physical goods is expensive and slow. Tokenization does not eliminate these business lines. It eliminates the friction that justifies the margin.

A bank that has tokenized its settlement layer, its deposit base, and its asset portfolio operates at a structurally lower cost than one that hasn’t. It can offer better yield, faster settlement, and richer product features — inside the same regulatory wrapper, with the same deposit insurance, the same legal recourse.

That is not a crypto competitor. That is a bank. And it is the most dangerous competitor an incumbent financial institution can face.

The Window Is Shorter Than It Looks#

Infrastructure transitions in financial services appear slow and then become sudden. The institutions that moved early on real-time payment rails didn’t look visionary at the time — they looked like they were spending money on plumbing. Until they weren’t.

Tokenization is at the same inflection point. The experimentation phase is ending. The operational phase has begun. BlackRock, Franklin Templeton, JPMorgan, and a growing list of institutions are not experimenting. They are settling real money on tokenized infrastructure at scale.

The question for every bank that hasn’t moved is not whether to tokenize. It is whether the decision to wait was made deliberately, with a clear-eyed view of the timeline — or by default, because it was easy to defer.

Default decisions in infrastructure are expensive. In financial services, they are occasionally existential.
