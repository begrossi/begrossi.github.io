The Clock Problem: Why Every CBDC Being Built Today Has a Security Expiration Date

Post-quantum cryptography isn't a future problem. It's a design decision being made right now — and most CBDC programs are getting it wrong.
August 1, 2024 · Bruno Grossi

There is a class of attack with an uncomfortable name: harvest now, decrypt later.

The premise is simple. A sophisticated adversary — a nation-state, a well-resourced criminal organization — intercepts and stores encrypted financial data today. They cannot read it. That’s fine. They are patient. They store it and wait for quantum computers capable of breaking current encryption to become available. When that day comes, years or decades from now, they decrypt everything retroactively.

Every transaction. Every counterparty. Every balance.

This is not a theoretical concern. It is an active intelligence strategy. And it is especially dangerous for one specific type of financial infrastructure: central bank digital currencies.

Why CBDCs Are a Unique Target#

CBDCs are not cryptocurrencies. They are sovereign money — a direct digital liability of the central bank, with legal tender status. When Brazil’s Real Digital processes a transaction, that record carries a different weight than a DeFi swap on a public blockchain.

That weight makes it a target.

The cryptographic schemes protecting most CBDC infrastructure today — RSA, ECDSA, the elliptic curve signatures used in virtually every blockchain-derived system — are vulnerable to Shor’s algorithm, a quantum computation method capable of factoring large integers exponentially faster than any classical computer. The moment a sufficiently powerful quantum computer exists, these schemes break.

The financial community generally treats this as a distant problem. It is not.

NIST spent eight years evaluating post-quantum cryptographic algorithms. In 2024, they finalized three standards: ML-KEM (for key encapsulation), ML-DSA and SLH-DSA (for digital signatures). These algorithms are resistant to both classical and quantum attacks. They exist. They are standardized. The engineering to deploy them is non-trivial but tractable.

The question is not whether to migrate. It is when — and whether the systems being built today are designed to make that migration possible.

What the Research Found#

In July 2024, I co-authored an analysis published in the Cryptology ePrint Archive examining exactly this question in the context of DLT-based CBDC infrastructure: can post-quantum algorithms be applied to the signing and verification operations that underpin a CBDC system, and at what cost?

The findings were instructive — and in some cases, uncomfortable.

The good news: post-quantum signature schemes can be integrated into DLT-based CBDC infrastructure. The algorithms are mature enough for production consideration. The feasibility is real.

The harder news: the performance profile is different. Post-quantum signatures are significantly larger than their classical equivalents. ML-DSA signatures are roughly 10–50× larger than ECDSA, depending on the security level chosen. SLH-DSA is even larger, though more conservative. On a high-throughput payment rail — the kind a central bank digital currency needs — this has implications for network bandwidth, storage, and transaction validation latency.

These are solvable engineering problems. But they require design choices made now, not during a post-quantum migration panic in 2031.

The Actual Risk Model#

Here is what most CBDC programs are not saying publicly: the threat window for harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks is already open.

Transactions processed on Real Digital today will exist as permanent cryptographic records. If those records are stored — by any sufficiently motivated party — and if RSA or ECDSA is the underlying signature scheme, then the confidentiality of those transactions is contingent on quantum computers never reaching cryptographic relevance.

That is a bet. It may be a reasonable bet for the next five years. It is a much less reasonable bet for the next twenty.

The systems being architected today will likely still be processing transactions in 2040. The cryptographic assumptions built into them will be load-bearing for that entire period. Getting them wrong is not an upgrade problem — it is a redesign problem.

What Responsible Design Looks Like#

Cryptographic agility — the ability to swap signature schemes without rebuilding the underlying infrastructure — is the architectural decision that separates CBDCs built to last from CBDCs built to ship.

It means abstracting the signature layer. It means not hardcoding ECDSA into the consensus protocol. It means having a migration path that doesn’t require reissuing every credential in the system.

It is not glamorous engineering. It rarely makes the press release. But it is the difference between a financial system that can adapt to a post-quantum world and one that becomes a liability the moment the threat materializes.

Brazil’s Real Digital has the opportunity to get this right — to become not just a technically functional CBDC, but a reference implementation for post-quantum-ready sovereign digital money.

That window is open. It does not stay open indefinitely.
